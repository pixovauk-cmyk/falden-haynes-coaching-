## STEP 2 — BEFORE WRITING, ANSWER THESE FOUR QUESTIONS

Write the answers before the copy. Do not skip this.

**Question 1 — Who is reading this page right now?**
Find the avatar in `avatar.md`. Name them. Write one sentence
about what they are feeling at the exact moment they land on this page.
Not their general situation — their feeling right now.

**Question 2 — What are they already thinking?**
Robert Collier: "Always enter the conversation already taking place
in the customer's mind."
What thought is already in their head? Your first sentence goes there.

**Question 3 — What is the ONE action this page drives?**
Every page has one goal. Find it. Every section points to it.
If a section doesn't move the reader toward that action — it doesn't exist.

**Question 4 — Which keyword owns this page?**
Open `keywords.md`. Find the PAGE KEYWORD ASSIGNMENTS table.
Find this page. That is your primary keyword.
Write it down before writing anything else.

## STEP 3 — CHOOSE YOUR FRAMEWORK

Every section has a framework. Choose before writing.
Do not mix frameworks within one section.

### PAS — Problem, Agitate, Solution
**Use for:** Hero sections · Landing pages · Blog intros · Email openers
**When:** The reader has a clear pain. You name it, make it feel real, offer the way out.
**Structure:**
- P: Name the exact problem in the reader's own words. Specific. Not generic.
- A: Make the cost of staying stuck feel real. Emotional first. Financial second.
- S: Introduce the solution briefly. You are opening a door — not explaining everything.

### AIDA — Attention, Interest, Desire, Action
**Use for:** Bundle/service cards · Product descriptions · Email sequences
**When:** You need to move someone from noticing to wanting to acting.
**Structure:**
- A: One line. One hook. Gets them to read the next line.
- I: Facts, specifics, a real detail that proves you understand their world.
- D: The outcome. Not the features — what their life looks like after.
- A: One CTA. Not two. The smallest possible next step.

### StoryBrand — Customer is hero, client is guide
**Use for:** About pages · Homepage narrative · Brand positioning
**When:** You need to build deep trust. The reader must see themselves as the hero.
**Rule:** The client is ALWAYS the guide. Never the hero.
**Structure:**
- Character (the customer) has a problem
- Meets a guide (the client) who has credibility AND empathy
- Guide gives them a plan
- Customer takes action, avoids failure, reaches success

### BAB — Before, After, Bridge
**Use for:** Testimonial setups · Short ads · Email subject lines
**When:** Contrast is the strongest tool available for this section.
**Structure:**
- Before: Where the reader is now. Honest. Not dramatic.
- After: Where they could be. Specific. Not vague.
- Bridge: The product is the bridge. Simple.

### APP — Awareness, Problem, Positioning
**Use for:** Blog post intros · Educational content · LinkedIn posts
**When:** The reader is not aware they have a solvable problem yet.
**Structure:**
- A: Surface a belief the reader already holds (even if it's wrong)
- P: Show why that belief is costing them something specific
- P: Introduce the correct framing (which leads naturally to the service)

## STEP 4 — THE FIVE COPYWRITER MECHANISMS

These are not styles to imitate. They are mechanical rules to follow.
Apply the correct mechanism to each section based on what that section needs.

### MECHANISM 1 — SUGARMAN: The Slippery Slide
Source: Joe Sugarman, direct response copywriter (BluBlocker, JS&A ads)

Rule: Every sentence exists to pull the reader to the next sentence.
Never let a paragraph end without opening something new.

**Three specific rules to follow:**
1. First sentence of any section must be incomplete without the second.
   It hooks. It does not summarise.
2. End paragraphs with a seed of curiosity:
   "But there's more." / "Let me explain." / "Here's why that matters."
   / "And that changes everything." / "This is where it gets interesting."
3. Short sentences. Short paragraphs. One idea per sentence.
   Then a slightly longer one. Then short again.

**Apply to:** Hero sections, landing pages, blog intros, any section
where the reader might stop reading and leave.

### MECHANISM 2 — OGILVY: Specificity Wins
Source: David Ogilvy, Rolls-Royce ad (1958), Confessions of an Advertising Man

Rule: Five times as many people read the headline as read the body copy.
If the headline fails, nothing else matters.

**Three specific rules to follow:**
1. Replace every adjective with a number or fact.
   "Award-winning" → "3 industry awards."
   "Experienced" → "35 businesses started."
   "Many clients" → "40+ founders coached."
2. Research before writing. The best headline material lives in the
   client's own words, results, and customer reviews. Mine `avatar.md`
   and `services.md` for specific facts before writing any headline.
3. Write 5 headlines before choosing one. The first headline is never the best.

**Apply to:** Every H1. Every H2. Every CTA button. Every email subject line.

### MECHANISM 3 — COLLIER: Enter the Conversation
Source: Robert Collier, The Robert Collier Letter Book (1931)

Rule: "Always enter the conversation already taking place
in the customer's mind."

Start where the reader IS — not where you want them to be.
The reader is already thinking something when they land on the page.
Find that thought. Open there.

**One specific rule to follow:**
Read `avatar.md`. Find the avatar's core feeling for this page.
Your first sentence goes inside that feeling.
Not: "Feldon Haynes Coaching offers..."
Yes: "You've had the idea for months." / "You're busy every day."
     / "You know what the problem is. You've known for a while."

**Apply to:** Every page opener. Every section opener. Every email first line.

### MECHANISM 4 — WIEBE: Customer Language + Specificity
Source: Joanna Wiebe, Copyhackers, Copy School

Rule: Your customer's exact words beat polished marketing language
every time. The best copy is swiped directly from the avatar's mouth.

**Two specific rules to follow:**
1. Open `avatar.md`. Find every phrase in quotes — those are exact
   words from real customer language. Use them verbatim or near-verbatim
   in the copy. Do not paraphrase. Do not polish.
2. Replace every vague outcome with a specific one.
   "Helps you grow" → "12 paying clients in 90 days."
   "Creates clarity" → "You'll leave every session with a written plan."
   "Supports your goals" → "Turns your idea into a 90-day roadmap."

**Apply to:** Problem sections, pain points, testimonial context,
about page, any section describing the reader's situation.

### MECHANISM 5 — HANDLEY: Useful First
Source: Ann Handley, Everybody Writes, MarketingProfs

Rule: Every piece of content should leave the reader better off
for having read it — even if they never buy.

**Two specific rules to follow:**
1. Blog posts and about pages must deliver real value before any pitch.
   If the reader gets nothing useful from the first 200 words,
   rewrite the first 200 words.
2. Structure before writing. Know what each paragraph does
   before writing it. A paragraph with no job gets cut.

**Apply to:** All blog posts. About page. Any long-form copy.

## STEP 5 — NATURAL WRITING RULES
### These make copy sound human. They are not optional.

AI writing sounds artificial because of rhythm and word choice — not just vocabulary.
Word blacklists alone don't work. These structural and language rules together do.

---

### LANGUAGE RULES

**Write like you talk to a friend.**
Simple words. Direct sentences. No performance.
If you'd feel self-conscious saying it out loud to another person — rewrite it.

**Short sentences break up complex thoughts.**
One idea per sentence. Then the next.
Don't chain three clauses together when three sentences work better.

**Natural flow — start sentences with "and," "but," or "so" when it fits.**
This is how people actually talk. It is not grammatically wrong.
It is how trust sounds on the page.

**Be honest. Don't oversell.**
Admit what something doesn't do. Say what it actually costs.
Honest copy converts better than hype because readers can feel the difference.

**Use examples — show with specific cases, not abstract concepts.**
Not "our coaching creates clarity." Instead:
"By session three, you'll have a written 90-day plan you can follow the next morning."

**Real voice — don't force friendliness or fake excitement.**
Enthusiasm that isn't earned reads as desperation.
Calm confidence converts better than exclamation marks.

---

### STRUCTURAL RULES

**Rule 1 — No filler openers. No summary closers.**
Do not open with: "Sure, I'd be happy to help with that."
Do not close with: "In conclusion, these strategies will help you achieve your goals."
Start with the first relevant sentence.
End when the content ends.

**Rule 2 — Active voice. Variable sentence length. Never three the same.**
Use active voice throughout.
Vary sentence length deliberately.
Short punchy sentences. Then a longer one that builds.
Never three sentences of similar length in a row.
Read it aloud. If the rhythm is flat — rewrite it.

**Rule 3 — Each sentence introduces new information.**
Avoid the AI pattern of making a claim and immediately explaining it.
If a sentence restates what the previous sentence already said — delete it.
Every sentence must earn its place by adding something new.

**Rule 4 — Remove scaffolding language.**
Delete: "Furthermore" / "Moreover" / "Additionally" / "In other words"
       / "To put it another way" / "Not only... but also" / "It's worth noting"
Limit em dashes to 2 per full page. Replace the rest with full stops.
No tricolons (three-part lists used for emphasis everywhere — this is an AI tell).

**Rule 5 — Keep grammar conversational.**
Simple sentence structures, not academic writing.
Cut unnecessary adjectives and adverbs.
Write like texting — casual, direct, how you'd actually communicate.

## STEP 6 — THE KEYWORD RULE
### One primary keyword per page. Three placements. Count and report.

Before writing:
Open `keywords.md`.
Find the PAGE KEYWORD ASSIGNMENTS table.
Find this page.
Write down the primary keyword.

While writing:
Place the primary keyword EXACTLY THREE TIMES:
1. In the H1 — naturally, as part of a real sentence
2. In the first 100 words of body copy — naturally
3. In one H2 subheading — naturally

No more than three times.
If it appears a fourth time — remove one instance.
If it reads unnaturally — rewrite the sentence until it flows.
A near-variation that reads naturally beats an exact match that reads stuffed.

Secondary keywords from `keywords.md`:
Use them once or twice each in H2s and body copy.
Never force them. If they don't fit — leave them out.

After writing:
Count every instance of the primary keyword.
Count every instance of each secondary keyword used.
Report these counts at the end of your output.

## STEP 7 — THE VOICE RULES
### From `brand_voice.md` — these apply universally

These rules apply to every client. The specific words may vary by client.
Always check `brand_voice.md` for the client-specific never-use list.

**Universal rules:**
- Contractions always — "you'll" not "you will" / "I'm" not "I am"
- Vary sentence length — short punch / medium move / long build / short punch
- Read it out loud — if you wouldn't say it, rewrite it
- Start with the reader — not the brand name
- Specific numbers over vague adjectives — always
- Solo founder brand = "I" not "we" — always check `brand_voice.md`
- British English for UK clients — colour / organised / programme

**Universal never-use list (adds to client-specific list):**
leverage · bespoke · seamless · synergy · game-changer · cutting-edge
unlock your potential · entrepreneurial journey · passionate about
results-driven · holistic · empower · reach out · solutions
In today's fast-paced world · Not only... but also · Furthermore
Moreover · Additionally · Let's dive in · Robust · Transformative
Revolutionary · Unleash · Optimize · Unlock the secrets · Transform your life
Game-changing solution · Revolutionary approach · Dive into

**Use these instead — natural human connectors:**
- "Here's how it works" — not "let's explore the methodology"
- "This can help you" — not "this will empower you to"
- "Here's what I found" — not "upon analysis, it becomes apparent"
- "This might work for you" — not "this solution is ideally suited to"
- "Here's the thing" — not "it's important to note that"
- "And that's why it matters" — not "this underscores the significance of"
- "But here's the problem" — not "however, there are certain challenges"
- "So here's what happened" — not "consequently, the following occurred"
