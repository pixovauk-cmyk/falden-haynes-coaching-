## HOW TO USE THIS FILE

Do not read this as a style guide.
Read it as a mechanics manual.

Every section shows you a MECHANISM — a specific thing that makes copy
pull the reader forward. Study the annotation. Understand WHY it works.
Then apply the same mechanism to the client's copy — not the same words.

When you write a section, ask: which mechanism does this section need?
Find that mechanism here. See how it works. Build your version.

## PART 1 — THE RHYTHM GUIDE
### How human copy actually flows

This is the most important thing to understand before writing a single word.
AI writing sounds artificial not because it uses the wrong words
but because it uses the right words in the wrong rhythm.

Human writing is BURSTY. Sentences bounce between short and long.
AI writing is FLAT. Every sentence is 15-22 words. Same beat. Same structure.

**Here is the same paragraph written flat (AI) vs bursty (human):**

FLAT — AI default:
```
Starting a business can be a challenging and overwhelming experience,
particularly for first-time founders who are navigating the complex
landscape of entrepreneurship without the guidance of an experienced
mentor or coach to help them structure their ideas and create
actionable plans for moving forward.
```
[One sentence. 53 words. One beat. Monotonous. Nobody reads past this.]

BURSTY — human:
```
Starting a business is hard.
[5 words. Full stop. Lands hard.]

Not because the idea is bad.
[7 words. Reframes the assumption.]

Because nobody shows you how to turn what's in your head
into something you can actually build.
[20 words. The longer sentence earns its length because it delivers a real insight.]

That's the gap.
[3 words. Short punch. Forces the reader to pause.]

And that's exactly what a good coach fixes.
[9 words. Resolution. Opens next question — what coach?]
```

**ANNOTATION KEY:**
- Short sentence (under 8 words) = PUNCH. Creates emphasis. Forces a pause.
- Medium sentence (8-15 words) = MOVE. Carries information forward.
- Long sentence (15+ words) = BUILD. Used for payoff, insight, or evidence. Never more than one in a row.
- After a long sentence — always a short one.
- Never three sentences of the same length in a row. Ever.

## PART 2 — THE SUGARMAN MECHANISM
### Source: Joe Sugarman — BluBlocker sunglasses, JS&A direct response ads (1970s-80s)

Slippery slide copy: "Your readers should be so compelled to read
your copy that they cannot stop reading until they read all of it
as if sliding down a slippery slide."

**The mechanism has three rules:**

**Rule 1 — The first sentence exists only to pull the reader to the second.**
It is not a summary. It is not an introduction. It is a hook with an open loop.

**Rule 2 — Seeds of curiosity.**
End paragraphs with short teases that give readers a reason
to keep reading — "But there's more" / "Let me explain" /
"Here's why that matters" / "And that changes everything."

**Rule 3 — Short words, short sentences, short paragraphs. Then vary.**
Most copy should consist of short words, short sentences, and short
paragraphs — because they make it much easier to read.
But mix it up. Longer sentences stop the rhythm from feeling monotonous.

---

**REAL EXAMPLE — Sugarman mechanism (from BluBlocker ad):**
```
"I'm about to tell you about one of the most unusual sunglasses
I've ever had the privilege of offering."
```
[Opens mid-thought. "I'm about to tell you" creates anticipation.
"Most unusual" raises a question. Reader has to know what unusual means.]

```
"The sunglasses are called BluBlockers."
```
[Short. Names the product. Matter-of-fact. Earns trust by being plain.]

```
"And what makes them unusual is the way they make you see."
```
[Seeds the next question — how do they make you see differently?
Reader cannot stop without finding out.]

---

**FABRICATED SERVICE BUSINESS EXAMPLE — Sugarman mechanism:**

Applied to a business coaching context:

```
"Most founders who come to me have already tried to fix this themselves."
```
[Opens with a surprising fact about the reader. Not about the coach.
"Already tried" acknowledges their effort. Reader nods — that's me.]

```
"They've watched the videos. Read the books. Downloaded the templates."
```
[Rhythm: three short parallel phrases. Each one lands separately.
Reader nods three times. Trust builds.]

```
"None of it worked. Not because they were doing it wrong."
```
[Short punch. Then a reframe that surprises. Reader stops and thinks.]

```
"Because they were missing the one thing none of those resources give you."
```
[Opens a loop. What is the one thing? Reader must keep reading.]

```
"A plan that's actually built for their specific business."
```
[Closes the loop. Short. Clear. But immediately raises the next question:
how do I get that? Slide continues.]

---

**CURSOR QUESTION — what to ask before writing any section using Sugarman:**
- What is the one question this paragraph should leave unanswered?
- Does my first sentence work without the second sentence? If yes, rewrite it.
- Have I ended this section with something that pulls them forward?

## PART 3 — THE OGILVY MECHANISM
### Source: David Ogilvy — Rolls-Royce ad (1958), Confessions of an Advertising Man

Ogilvy knew that on average, five times as many people read the
headline as read the body copy. If the headline fails, nothing else matters.

Ogilvy's most famous headline was directly swiped from a quote
he read in a British motor magazine. He knew how to swipe and use inspiration.
Modern copywriters reference Amazon reviews the same way.

**The mechanism has three rules:**

**Rule 1 — The headline carries 80% of the work. Spend 80% of your time on it.**
If the headline doesn't stop someone scrolling, nothing else matters.

**Rule 2 — Specificity is credibility. Numbers beat adjectives every time.**
"In my first Rolls-Royce advertisement, I used 719 words,
piling one fascinating fact on another."
Not "many facts." 719 facts. Specific.

**Rule 3 — Research before writing.**
Ogilvy spent three weeks researching the Rolls-Royce before
writing a single word of copy. The headline came from a quote in a motor
magazine — not from his imagination.
The client's own words, numbers, and results are the raw material.

---

**REAL EXAMPLE — Ogilvy mechanism (Rolls-Royce headline):**
```
"At 60 miles an hour the loudest noise in this new Rolls-Royce
comes from the electric clock."
```
[No adjectives. No "luxury." No "premium." One specific fact that
communicates everything. The reader feels the silence.
The specificity — 60mph, electric clock — makes it undeniable.]

---

**FABRICATED SERVICE BUSINESS EXAMPLE — Ogilvy mechanism:**

Weak version (adjective-led):
```
"Award-winning business coach helping ambitious founders
achieve their goals."
```
[Meaningless. Every coach says this. Nothing to hold onto.]

Ogilvy version (specific fact-led):
```
"35 businesses started. 40 founders coached.
3 industry awards. One free discovery call."
```
[Four facts. No adjectives. Nothing to argue with.
The last line is a CTA that feels earned — not pushed.]

Another Ogilvy version for a headline:
```
"The business coach who's actually run 35 businesses."
```
[One specific differentiator. Instantly credible.
Every competing coach disappears against this line.]

---

**CURSOR QUESTION — what to ask before writing any headline:**
- Can I replace any adjective with a specific number or fact?
- Would this headline stop me if I was scrolling?
- Have I used any of these words: award-winning, passionate, results-driven,
  bespoke, leading, innovative? If yes — delete and replace with a fact.

## PART 4 — THE COLLIER MECHANISM
### Source: Robert Collier — The Robert Collier Letter Book (1931)

"Always enter the conversation already taking place
in the customer's mind." — Robert Collier

There are already ideas, conversations, and thoughts prominent
in your customers' minds. To reach them, tap into those existing
thoughts and ride the coattails of what has their attention.

Most rookie copywriters start with their message. Their product.
Their pitch. But Collier focused on what the reader was already thinking,
feeling, worrying about, or dreaming of. He matched the message to
the reader's mindset — not the other way around.

"If your reader is stressed about money and you open with 'Let me tell
you about our advanced software solution,' you've already lost them.
But if you start with 'If you're sick of checking your bank account
before buying groceries' — now you've got their attention."

**The mechanism has one rule:**
Start in the middle of the reader's current thought — not at the beginning of your pitch.

---

**REAL EXAMPLE — Collier mechanism:**

Wrong (starts with the pitch):
```
"Feldon Haynes Coaching Services offers 1-to-1 business coaching
for ambitious founders and small business owners across the UK."
```
[Reader is not thinking about Feldon. They are thinking about their problem.
This copy arrives in the wrong conversation entirely.]

Right (enters the reader's thought):
```
"You've had the idea for six months.
Maybe longer.
You know it's good. You just don't know where to start."
```
[This is already happening in Jordan's head when they land on the page.
The copy finds them where they are. They feel understood before they've
been sold anything. Trust begins here.]

---

**FABRICATED SERVICE BUSINESS EXAMPLES — Collier mechanism by avatar:**

For Jordan (aspiring founder, stuck before launch):
```
"You're not stuck because you're not smart enough.
You know that.
You're stuck because no one has helped you turn what's in your head
into something you can actually build from."
```

For Marcus (founder who has lost direction):
```
"You're busy every day.
But busy isn't the same as building.
And somewhere in the last six months, you stopped feeling
like you were building anything."
```

For Diane (established business, specific challenge):
```
"You know what the problem is.
You've known for a while.
What you need is someone outside the business
who can help you see it clearly enough to actually fix it."
```

---

**CURSOR QUESTION — what to ask before writing any page opener:**
- What is the reader already thinking RIGHT NOW as they land on this page?
- Does my first sentence find them in that thought — or does it announce the brand?
- Read `avatar.md`. Find the avatar's core feeling. Start there.

## PART 5 — THE WIEBE MECHANISM
### Source: Joanna Wiebe — Copyhackers, Copy School (2011-present)

Voice of customer language is au naturale. Unvarnished.
It sounds more authentic because it IS. And that makes it more potent
than marketing speak.

The more specific your copy, the more believable and persuasive it becomes.
Shift from "We help you grow your business" to "We helped a London-based
creative agency generate 143 qualified leads in 12 months" — and suddenly
people lean in. Specificity tells the prospect: this is real, this has
happened, this could work for you too.

**The mechanism has two rules:**

**Rule 1 — Mine the avatar's exact words. Use them verbatim.**
Look in `avatar.md` for phrases in quotes. Those are the words.
Do not paraphrase. Do not polish. Drop them directly into the copy.

**Rule 2 — Replace every vague claim with a specific outcome.**
"I help founders" → "I've sat with 40+ founders, most stuck at exactly your point"
"I have experience" → "I've started 35 businesses"
"Great results" → "12 paying clients in the first 90 days"

---

**REAL EXAMPLE — Wiebe mechanism:**

Joanna Wiebe tested a headline for a rehab centre.
The control: "Your addiction ends here."
The challenger: "If you think you need rehab, you do."

The challenger was swiped verbatim from a book review written by
someone in recovery. It dramatically outperformed the professional headline
because it used the exact words the reader was already thinking.

---

**FABRICATED SERVICE BUSINESS EXAMPLE — Wiebe mechanism:**

Avatar words from `avatar.md` (Jordan):
- "I don't know where to start"
- "I've been thinking about this for months"
- "What if I do all this work and it fails"

Weak copy (polished marketing version):
```
"Our coaching helps aspiring founders overcome the barriers to launch
and create structured plans for business success."
```
[Sanitised. Not one word Jordan would use.]

Wiebe version (customer language used directly):
```
"You've been thinking about this for months.
Maybe longer.
You don't know where to start. And you're worried that if you do —
it won't work."
```
[Every phrase is Jordan's own language. She reads this and thinks:
how do they know? That's me exactly. Trust starts here.]

---

**CURSOR QUESTION — what to ask before writing any pain or problem section:**
- Open `avatar.md`. Find the avatar's exact quotes. Are they in this copy?
- Have I replaced every vague claim with a specific number or outcome?
- Would the avatar read this and think "that's exactly what I'd say"?

## PART 6 — THE HANDLEY MECHANISM
### Source: Ann Handley — Everybody Writes, MarketingProfs (2014-present)

**The mechanism: Useful first. Persuasive second. Structure always.**

Every piece of content should leave the reader better off for having read it —
even if they never buy. The trust built by being genuinely useful is what
eventually converts. Not the cleverness of the copy.

**Three rules:**

**Rule 1 — Pathological empathy.**
Understand the reader's situation so completely that they feel understood
before you say a single word about what you offer.

**Rule 2 — Structure is what separates readable from unreadable.**
Plan the flow before writing. Know what each paragraph does before writing it.
A structured draft is always better than an inspired but shapeless one.

**Rule 3 — Every blog post should be worth reading even if they never hire you.**
If the content only makes sense as a sales pitch, it is not content — it is an ad.
Good content earns trust. Ads demand attention. Trust converts better.

---

**FABRICATED SERVICE BUSINESS EXAMPLE — Handley mechanism:**

Applied to a blog post intro:

Weak (ad disguised as content):
```
"If you want to write a business plan, you need to hire a coach.
At Feldon Haynes Coaching, we help founders build plans that work.
Book your free discovery call today."
```
[This is a sales pitch. The reader came for information.
They leave immediately.]

Handley version (useful first):
```
"A business plan doesn't need to be 40 pages.
It doesn't need financial projections you've made up.
And it doesn't need to impress anyone.

It needs to answer three questions: what are you building,
who is it for, and how do you make money from it.
That's it.

This post walks you through how to answer all three —
honestly, quickly, and in plain English."
```
[Reader gets real value in the first paragraph.
No pitch yet. Trust is being built.
By the end, when the CTA appears, it feels earned — not forced.]

---

**CURSOR QUESTION — what to ask before writing any blog post or about page:**
- Would the reader be better off for having read this, even if they never book?
- Have I delivered real value before making any kind of pitch?
- Is the structure planned — do I know what each section does?

## PART 7 — BEFORE AND AFTER REWRITES
### Six examples showing exactly what changes and why

---

**REWRITE 1 — Hero headline**

BEFORE (AI default):
```
"Welcome to Feldon Haynes Coaching — empowering ambitious founders
to achieve their entrepreneurial goals through bespoke 1-to-1 coaching."
```
Problems:
- Opens with "Welcome to" — never do this
- "Empowering" — meaningless filler verb
- "Bespoke" — everyone says this, says nothing
- "Entrepreneurial goals" — vague. What goals?
- Starts with the brand, not the reader

AFTER (human, Collier opener + Ogilvy specificity):
```
"You've got the idea. You just need the plan."
```
Why it works:
- 9 words. Punchy. Complete.
- Starts in the reader's head — not with the brand name
- No adjectives
- Creates an immediate question: what plan? how?

---

**REWRITE 2 — About page opener**

BEFORE (AI default):
```
"Feldon Haynes is an award-winning entrepreneur and certified business coach
with over a decade of experience helping founders and small business owners
across the West Midlands and UK to achieve their goals and reach
their full potential."
```
Problems:
- Starts with the client's name — nobody cares yet
- "Award-winning" — vague
- "Certified" — implies the credential matters more than the results
- "Full potential" — the most meaningless phrase in coaching
- Passive, third person, zero personality

AFTER (human, Collier + Ogilvy + Sugarman):
```
"I've started 35 businesses.
Some flew. Some didn't.
Either way — I learned something useful.
And now I put all of it into every session."
```
Why it works:
- First person — personal brand, solo founder
- Specific number — 35. Not "many." Not "numerous."
- Honest about failure — builds more trust than a highlight reel
- Seeds curiosity — what did you learn?
- Short punchy rhythm — easy to read, hard to forget

---

**REWRITE 3 — Bundle description**

BEFORE (AI default):
```
"The Business Plan Bundle is a comprehensive coaching programme designed
to take aspiring entrepreneurs through the key stages of business planning,
from initial concept validation through to operational setup and launch
readiness, delivered across six personalised sessions."
```
Problems:
- 46 words. One sentence. No rhythm.
- "Comprehensive" — says nothing
- "Key stages" — vague
- No outcomes. No emotion. No specifics.
- Sounds like a brochure

AFTER (human, Sugarman + Wiebe):
```
"Six sessions. One complete business plan.

We start with your idea — is it viable, is there a real market,
does the business model actually make money?
Then we build it. Model, positioning, operations, launch plan,
90-day roadmap.

You leave every session with a written action plan.
Not more questions. An actual plan."
```
Why it works:
- "Six sessions. One complete business plan." — short. Specific. Immediate.
- Each sentence introduces new information — no repetition
- Ends with the outcome the buyer actually wants
- "Not more questions. An actual plan." — addresses the objection
  every coaching sceptic has before you voice it

---

**REWRITE 4 — Problem section card**

BEFORE (AI default):
```
"Many aspiring entrepreneurs find themselves struggling to take the
first steps towards launching their business due to a lack of structure,
guidance, and actionable frameworks to help them move forward."
```
Problems:
- "Many aspiring entrepreneurs" — not one person. Generic.
- "Lack of structure, guidance, and actionable frameworks" — consultant speak
- Passive voice throughout
- No emotion. No specificity. Nobody feels seen.

AFTER (human, Collier + Wiebe):
```
"You've got the idea. You haven't launched it.
It's been sitting there for months.
You believe in it. You just don't know where to actually start."
```
Why it works:
- Second person throughout — "you" 4 times in 3 sentences
- Uses the avatar's exact internal monologue
- Specific situation — not general struggle
- Short sentences — each one nods along with the reader

---

**REWRITE 5 — CTA section**

BEFORE (AI default):
```
"Don't hesitate to get in touch with us today to schedule your
complimentary discovery consultation and find out how our coaching
services can help you achieve your business goals."
```
Problems:
- "Don't hesitate" — weak opener. Starts with a negative.
- "Us" — solo founder. Should be "me."
- "Complimentary discovery consultation" — cold, clinical, corporate
- "Business goals" — vague

AFTER (human, Sugarman seed + Ogilvy specificity):
```
"The free 30-minute call costs you nothing.
Staying stuck for another six months might.

No pitch. No pressure. Just a conversation about where you are
and where you want to get to.

Pick a time that works for you."
```
Why it works:
- Opens with the benefit — free
- Second sentence creates urgency without being pushy or salesy
- "No pitch. No pressure." — removes the objection before it's raised
- "Pick a time" — softest possible CTA. Lowest friction.

---

**REWRITE 6 — Testimonial setup line**

BEFORE (AI default):
```
"Our clients have experienced remarkable transformations through
our coaching programmes, achieving significant milestones in their
entrepreneurial journeys."
```
Problems:
- "Remarkable transformations" — empty superlative
- "Significant milestones" — says nothing
- "Entrepreneurial journeys" — cliché
- Passive, distanced, zero personality

AFTER (human, plain and direct):
```
"Here's what founders say after working with me."
```
Why it works:
- 8 words. Gets out of the way.
- "Founders" — specific, not "clients"
- "After working with me" — personal, first person
- Lets the testimonials do the work. Does not oversell them first.

## PART 8 — THE SELF-AUDIT CLAUDE RUNS BEFORE OUTPUTTING

Before delivering any copy, Claude must ask these questions internally.
Not as a checklist — as genuine questions about the output.

**The human writing test — run this first:**
Read the copy out loud to yourself as if talking to a friend.
Ask these five questions:
- Does it sound like something you'd actually say out loud?
- Does it use words a normal person would use?
- Does it feel genuine — no forced friendliness or fake excitement?
- Does it get to the point quickly — no warm-up paragraphs?
- Does it use natural connectors — "here's the thing" / "but here's the problem"
  / "so here's what happened" — rather than formal transitions?

If any answer is no — rewrite before moving on.

**On natural connectors — use these instead of formal transitions:**

| Instead of this | Use this |
|---|---|
| "Furthermore, it should be noted..." | "Here's the thing..." |
| "It's important to understand that..." | "Here's how it works" |
| "This will empower you to..." | "This can help you" |
| "Upon reflection, it becomes clear..." | "Here's what I found" |
| "This solution is ideally suited to..." | "This might work for you" |
| "This underscores the significance of..." | "And that's why it matters" |
| "However, there are challenges..." | "But here's the problem" |
| "Consequently, the following occurred..." | "So here's what happened" |

**On rhythm:**
Read the copy out loud. Are there three or more sentences in a row of similar length?
If yes — break one. Shorten one. The rhythm must vary.

**On the opener:**
Does the copy open with the reader's world or the client's name?
If it opens with the client — rewrite the first sentence.

**On specificity:**
Are there any adjectives that could be replaced with a number or fact?
Award-winning → 3 industry awards. Many founders → 40+ founders.

**On the Collier test:**
What is the reader thinking RIGHT NOW as they arrive on this page?
Is the first line already inside that thought? If not — rewrite it.

**On the Sugarman test:**
Does each paragraph end in a way that pulls to the next?
Is there at least one seed of curiosity in every section?

**On the keyword:**
Find the primary keyword assigned in `keywords.md` for this page.
Count how many times it appears. Should be exactly 3.
H1 once. First 100 words once. One H2 once. No more.
Report the count alongside the output.

**On AI tells — never-use words:**
Check for every word in both lists before outputting:

HARD NEVER-USE:
dive into · unleash · game-changing · revolutionary · transformative
leverage · optimize · unlock the secrets · unlock potential
bespoke · seamless · synergy · cutting-edge · holistic · empower
results-driven · passionate about · reach out · robust

STRUCTURAL NEVER-USE:
"Not only... but also" · "Furthermore" · "Moreover" · "Additionally"
"In other words" · "Let's dive in" · "In conclusion"
"It's important to note that" · "It goes without saying"
Three bullet points all starting with -ing words
Any sentence that restates what the previous sentence just said

**On the voice:**
Read `brand_voice.md` never-use list. Is any word from that list in the copy?
If yes — find and remove every instance before outputting.

**Output format:**
Always deliver copy in this order:
1. The copy itself (clearly labelled by section)
2. Human writing check (5 questions — answered yes or no)
3. Keyword count report
4. AI tell audit (what was found and fixed)
5. One line on which copywriter mechanism was used for each section
