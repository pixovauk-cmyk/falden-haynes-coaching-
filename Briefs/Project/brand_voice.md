## The one-line summary

**Write like a brilliant mate who's built 35 businesses and genuinely wants you to succeed.**

Not a corporate consultant. Not a life coach who speaks in affirmations. Not an Instagram motivational account. A real person with real experience who will tell you the truth and give you a plan.

---

## Character

Feldon's brand voice has four qualities. Every piece of content should have all four.

### 1. Direct
Say the thing. Don't circle it. Don't soften it into meaninglessness.

❌ "There are many factors to consider when embarking on your entrepreneurial journey."
✅ "You've got the idea. You need the plan. Let's build it."

Short sentences land harder than long ones. If a sentence needs a semicolon, it probably needs to be two sentences.

### 2. Honest
No fluff. No empty promises. No "10x your revenue in 30 days."

Feldon is credible because he tells the truth — including the uncomfortable bits. That a business plan doesn't guarantee success. That some ideas need changing before they're launched. That the work is on the client, not just the coach.

This honesty is a differentiator. Every other coach overpromises. Feldon doesn't.

❌ "Transform your business and unlock your full potential."
✅ "Every session ends with a written action plan. What you do with it is up to you."

### 3. Warm
Direct and honest doesn't mean cold. Feldon genuinely cares about the people he works with — and that has to come through.

Acknowledge the struggle before offering the solution. Recognise that starting a business is scary. That being stuck is frustrating. That investing in coaching when money is tight takes courage.

The warmth isn't performed — it's the natural result of actually giving a damn.

❌ "Ineffective business planning leads to startup failure."
✅ "Most founders aren't stuck because they're not smart enough. They're stuck because no one's helped them turn what's in their head into a plan."

### 4. Credible — without showing off
Feldon has real credentials: 35+ businesses started, 40+ founders supported, 3 industry awards including the EPIC Spirit Award. These matter. Use them.

But never lead with the awards. Lead with the problem. Let the credentials do the reassuring, not the selling.

❌ "As an award-winning entrepreneur with over 35 businesses under his belt, Feldon Haynes is uniquely positioned to..."
✅ "I've started 35 businesses. Some worked, some didn't. I know what the difference looks like — and I can help you see it in yours."

## Voice in practice

### Always "I" — never "we"
This is a personal brand. Feldon is a solo founder. "We" sounds corporate and dishonest.

✅ "I work with founders who..."
❌ "We offer bespoke coaching solutions..."

### British English throughout
- "Organised" not "organized"
- "Colour" not "color"
- "Programme" not "program"
- Dates: 18 May 2026, not May 18 2026

### Sentence rhythm
Vary length deliberately. Short sentences punch. Slightly longer ones give context and show you've thought things through. Then short again. This rhythm keeps readers moving.

❌ "Feldon Haynes Coaching Services provides structured, personalised, and outcomes-focused business coaching for ambitious founders and entrepreneurs at all stages of their journey, with a particular focus on the West Midlands startup ecosystem."
✅ "I coach founders. From first idea to first client — and everywhere in between. Based in the West Midlands. Working with ambitious people across the UK."

### Acknowledge the feeling before the solution
Every good piece of copy starts with the reader nodding. Show you understand their situation before you explain what you do about it.

Structure: Pain → Recognition → Solution → Outcome

Example:
- Pain: "You've got a business idea you believe in."
- Recognition: "But every time you sit down to actually plan it, you don't know where to start."
- Solution: "That's exactly what I'm here for."
- Outcome: "You'll leave our first session with a written 90-day plan."

## Words and phrases to use

| Use | Because |
|---|---|
| "founders" | More accurate and resonant than "entrepreneurs" for the primary audience |
| "plan" / "action plan" | The core deliverable — use it often |
| "clarity" | What almost every client wants |
| "real experience" | Differentiates from theoretical coaches |
| "I've built businesses" | Credentials through doing, not just advising |
| "West Midlands" | Local trust signal |
| "session" not "appointment" | More active, less clinical |
| "let's" | Collaborative — "let's build" "let's talk" |
| "honest" | Core value and differentiator |

---

## Words and phrases to never use

| Never use | Why |
|---|---|
| "leverage" | Corporate waffle |
| "synergy" | Meaningless |
| "bespoke" | Overused, signals pretentiousness |
| "game-changer" | Empty superlative |
| "seamless" | Says nothing |
| "cutting-edge" | Cliché |
| "unlock your potential" | Generic motivational poster territory |
| "entrepreneurial journey" | Sounds like an MBA textbook |
| "In today's fast-paced world" | Never open with this. Ever. |
| "I am passionate about" | Show it, don't say it |
| "results-driven" | Every coach claims this — meaningless |
| "holistic" | Vague and often misleading |
| "we" | Personal brand — always "I" |
| "solutions" | Consultant-speak |
| "reach out" | Slightly irritating. Use "get in touch" or "drop me a message" |

## Tone by context

### Website homepage
Confident and welcoming. Lead with the problem, move quickly to what Feldon does about it. Keep sections punchy. Every section should end with either a CTA or a clear transition to the next.

### About page
More personal. More story. Still direct — but this is where warmth really comes through. First person throughout. Real experiences. The awkward bits as well as the wins.

### Bundle/services descriptions
Practical and specific. Clients reading these are evaluating whether to spend money — give them exactly what they get. No vague promises. Specific deliverables. Outcome language.

### Contact page
Short and human. Remove friction. Make it easy. Not "Please don't hesitate to contact us" — just "The best first step is a free 30-minute call. Pick a time below."

### Landing page (ad traffic)
Even more stripped back than homepage. One problem. One solution. One action. No distractions. No clever writing — just clear writing.

### LinkedIn posts
Personal and conversational. Real stories. Real lessons. One idea per post. End with a question or a soft CTA — never a hard sell. Write the way you'd talk to someone at a networking event, not the way you'd write a press release.

### Email
Direct subject lines (not clever, not clickbaity). Get to the point in the first two sentences. One CTA per email — not four. Signed off as Feldon — never "The Team" or any group sign-off.

## The anti-AI checklist

Before publishing anything, run it through this. If you tick any of these, rewrite.

- [ ] Does it open with "In today's..."? → Rewrite the opener
- [ ] Are all the sentences roughly the same length? → Vary the rhythm
- [ ] Does it use 3+ words where 1 would do? → Cut it
- [ ] Are there bullet points that all start with a gerund? (-ing word) → Restructure
- [ ] Does it sound like it was written for "everyone"? → Pick one avatar and rewrite for them
- [ ] Is there a passive voice construction? → Make it active
- [ ] Does it describe a feeling without grounding it in a real situation? → Add a specific detail
- [ ] Does it promise transformation without specifying what changes? → Make the outcome concrete

## The test

Read the copy out loud. Does it sound like something a real, confident, warm human being would actually say?

If it sounds like a website, rewrite it.
If it sounds like a brochure, rewrite it.
If it sounds like Feldon talking to a founder he genuinely wants to help — publish it.
